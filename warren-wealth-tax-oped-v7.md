# Senator Warren Is Right About the Problem. But the Wealth Tax Debate Is Blocking Real Reform.

*By Matt Sly*

Last week, Senator Elizabeth Warren and 49 Democratic co-sponsors reintroduced the Ultra-Millionaire Tax Act: a 2% annual levy on fortunes above $50 million, 3% above $1 billion, $6.2 trillion in projected revenue over a decade. The stated goal is to make the ultra-rich pay their fair share.

Senator Warren is diagnosing the right disease. Billionaires pay lower effective tax rates than teachers. The "buy, borrow, die" strategy lets the ultra-wealthy live tax-free by borrowing against appreciated assets until death erases the tax bill entirely. Trusts and other vehicles route around the estate tax. This is indefensible.

But a wealth tax is the wrong prescription. And by spending political capital on it, progressives risk protecting the status quo by making real reform less likely.

**The complexity trap.**

The tax code is exhibit A for progressivism's complexity problem. Every provision was written to protect someone — a worthy cause, a vulnerable group, a struggling industry, a behavior worth encouraging. Stacked together over a century, these well-intentioned protections have produced a system that protects, most of all, the people who needed protection least. The ultra-wealthy can afford the lawyers to navigate 75,000 pages. A nurse with a side business cannot.

This is the pattern the Abundance conversation has been naming across housing, energy, and permitting: layers of good intentions producing outcomes nobody wanted. The tax code is the fiscal version of the same story. You don't fix it by adding another layer. You fix it by removing layers.

**We don't need more tax code. We need less.**

The federal tax code and its regulations span 75,000 pages of interdependent provisions. Americans spend $536 billion per year just complying with it. The tax gap — money legally owed but uncollected — exceeds $600 billion annually. That's not an enforcement failure. It's a complexity failure. The IRS literally cannot administer the system we've already built.

A wealth tax adds an entirely new tax type on top of this mess. It requires annual valuation of illiquid assets — private businesses, real estate, art collections — that are expensive, subjective, and gameable to appraise. It creates new planning opportunities for the same lawyers who got us here and a new bureaucratic infrastructure the IRS doesn't have. This is the same design pattern that gave us the Alternative Minimum Tax: a shadow system bolted on because the primary system doesn't work. The AMT now ensnares millions of upper-middle-class families while the truly wealthy route around it. Adding complexity to fight the consequences of complexity is a losing strategy.

**It won't pass, and that matters.**

Every European country that tried a wealth tax repealed it. France's version drove an estimated 42,000 millionaires out of the country before repeal in 2017. Sweden, Austria, and Denmark reached the same conclusion. The constitutional challenges alone — the Sixteenth Amendment authorizes income taxes, not wealth taxes — would tie this up in courts for years.

Warren knows this. The bill has been introduced since 2019. It has never received a committee vote. At some point, proposing legislation you know won't pass stops being advocacy and starts being an obstacle to reforms that could. Every news cycle spent on a wealth tax is a news cycle not spent closing the loopholes that actually let billionaires pay nothing.

**There's a better way.**

I am a software engineer and entrepreneur. I've built systems used by millions, and I know that complexity compounds. Every patch interacts with every other patch until the codebase becomes too tangled to fix or enforce. The tax code is America's most expensive technical debt. And like any accumulated technical debt, the answer is not another patch. It's a refactor.

The ultra-wealthy don't pay low rates because statutory rates are too low. They pay low rates because they've found ways to avoid ever *realizing* income. We don't need a new layer on top. We need to close the exits on the layer we have.

Here is the refactor, in five rules:

**One.** Every American gets a lifetime capital gains exemption of $2.5 million per person, $5 million per couple. Below that, gains are tax-free. Most Americans will never hit this ceiling. It protects the family business, the long-held home, the retirement portfolio.

**Two.** Above the exemption, gains phase up to ordinary income rates, reaching full parity at $10 million. Capital gains and wages get taxed the same at the top. The preferential rate that has done so much to widen inequality disappears where it matters.

**Three.** Death and gifts trigger realization. The stepped-up basis loophole — the mechanism that lets generational wealth pass untaxed forever — is eliminated. This replaces the estate tax with something that actually collects, through a mechanism the IRS already understands.

**Four.** Borrowing against appreciated assets triggers realization. This is the specific fix for "buy, borrow, die." If you take a loan against $100 million of appreciated stock, the IRS treats that as a sale event for tax purposes. No more living tax-free off collateral.

**Five.** The AMT is eliminated. The estate tax is eliminated. Roughly 10 to 15 percent of the Internal Revenue Code, by volume, goes away. Not because we're cutting taxes on the wealthy — we're raising them — but because the workarounds the old code required are no longer needed.

No new tax type. No annual valuations. No constitutional question mark. Every mechanism is one the IRS already enforces.

**About the revenue.**

Honest answer: revenue estimates for reforms this structural are uncertain, and anyone who quotes you a precise number is selling something. Our modeling points to roughly $120 billion per year in new revenue under moderate behavioral-response assumptions, with meaningful sensitivity to how aggressively the ultra-wealthy restructure around realization events. That's less than Warren's $620 billion per year, and it's worth saying so plainly. But Warren's number assumes a wealth tax that collects — one that doesn't get litigated into oblivion, doesn't trigger the exodus France saw, and doesn't drown in valuation disputes. A reform that passes, survives the courts, and runs on infrastructure the IRS already operates is worth more than a larger number on a bill that never moves.

**The bigger picture.**

Senator Warren is right that the system is broken. She is right that billionaires aren't paying their share. She is right that the status quo is indefensible. But the answer isn't another patch on a century of patches. It's a refactor — one that closes the exits, eliminates the shadow systems, and delivers a tax code a human being can actually understand.

Abundance isn't just about building more houses and more clean energy. It's about building a government that can do what it says it will do. A tax code that collects what it's owed, from the people who owe it, without consuming $536 billion a year in compliance costs, is the fiscal precondition for everything else progressives want to accomplish. You can't fund the future on a system that can't even audit the present.

Fewer rules. Fewer exits. More revenue. That is the reform this moment actually needs.
