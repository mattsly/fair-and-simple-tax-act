




Dear Mr. Reid,

I am a software entrepreneur and engineer based in Boston who has become obsessed with tax code design.

It sounds like a crazy pivot, but it’s actually quite logical: the tax code is an algorithm. I’ve spent twenty years designing, maintaining, and "refactoring" complex algorithms, and that is exactly what the U.S. tax code needs. I’ve come to see it as our country’s greatest piece of "technical debt", as we say in the software business.

As I was ramping up on the subject, the first thing I did was read A Fine Mess. It catalyzed my obsession.

My sensibility is that of a pragmatic centrist. I just want the system to be simpler. Simplicity makes loopholes evaporate and, more importantly, it enables a more honest discussion about cost and benefit. I suspect the opacity of the tax code is an unappreciated ingredient in the toxicity of our political dialogue. There is simply too much confusion to have a well-informed discussion.

I’ve started researching and writing about a "systems design" approach to reform. You can see the framework I'm building here: https://taxrefactor.substack.com

The Pitch: Next year marks the 10th anniversary of A Fine Mess. What would you think about brainstorming a follow-up? I think with the advent of AI (and what that may mean for labor vs capital), and the rumbling about wealth taxes and the continuted explosion of the deficit, tax policy has the potential to be a hot topic!

I’m envisioning: The Tax Refactor: Cleaning up the Fine Mess.
