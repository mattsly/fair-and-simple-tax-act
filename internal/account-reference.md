# The Alphabet Soup: A Reference Guide

Quick reference on every tax-advantaged savings account the USA proposal would replace. For internal use / boning up. Figures are the most recent available (2024–2025); contribution limits are 2025 unless noted. Anything marked *(approx.)* is a from-memory estimate worth re-checking before it goes in print.

**Tax-treatment shorthand:**
- **Pre-tax** = contributions deductible (or excluded from income) going in; growth deferred; withdrawals taxed as ordinary income. ("Traditional.")
- **Post-tax / Roth** = contributions made with already-taxed dollars; growth and qualified withdrawals tax-free.
- **Triple-tax-free** = deductible in, tax-free growth, tax-free out (HSAs only).

## By category, with context

### Workplace retirement (employer-sponsored)

**401(k) (Traditional & Roth).** The workhorse. Born almost by accident: §401(k) was a small 1978 provision, and benefits consultant Ted Benna realized in 1980 it could be used for a salary-deferral plan. Traditional is pre-tax; the Roth version (authorized 2001, live 2006) is post-tax. The employee elective limit ($23,500) is separate from the much higher combined employee-plus-employer limit ($70,000) — the gap the employer match lives in. The single biggest private retirement pool.

**403(b).** The oldest tax-advantaged retirement vehicle (1958), for public schools, universities, hospitals, churches, and nonprofits. Functionally a 401(k) for the nonprofit world; historically annuity-heavy and notorious for high fees.

**457(b).** Deferred compensation for state/local government employees and some nonprofits. Quirk: no early-withdrawal penalty once you leave the employer, and it can be stacked on top of a 403(b), letting some public employees defer doubly.

**TSP (Thrift Savings Plan).** The federal government's own 401(k), created with the modern federal retirement system in 1986. Famous for rock-bottom fees and a handful of simple index funds (the G/F/C/S/I funds). Often cited — including in this project — as proof that a clean, low-fee, few-options default works: $963B and 7.2M participants. In many ways the closest existing thing to the USA's design philosophy.

**Pensions (defined benefit).** The old model: the employer promises a monthly income in retirement and bears the investment risk. Largely frozen or closed in the private sector (~$3.2T and shrinking) but still dominant for state/local government workers (~$8.5T). The shift from DB pensions to DC accounts (401(k)s) is the larger story behind why individuals now shoulder retirement risk themselves.

### Individual retirement

**Traditional IRA.** Created by ERISA in 1974 for workers without a pension. Pre-tax, with income limits on deductibility if you also have a workplace plan. The largest single destination for retirement money (~$14.5T) — mostly because people roll old 401(k)s into IRAs when they change jobs.

**Roth IRA.** Senator William Roth's 1997 creation (effective 1998). Post-tax: no deduction going in, but tax-free forever after. Has income limits on who can contribute directly — the limit that spawned the "backdoor Roth." The template the USA is most directly modeled on.

**SEP & SIMPLE IRAs.** Two simplified employer plans for small businesses and the self-employed. SEP (1978) is employer-funded only, with a high limit (up to $70K). SIMPLE (1996) allows employee deferrals up to $16,500 with mandatory employer contributions, aimed at businesses under 100 employees. Both exist because full 401(k)s were too costly/complex for small shops — a tell that the system needed simplification, not more variants.

### Education

**529 plan.** The big one for education: $525B across 17 million accounts, average balance ~$31K. State-administered (50+ plans), post-tax federally but often state-tax-deductible, and completely tax-free for qualified education. Grew out of state prepaid-tuition programs (Michigan, 1986); §529 codified it in 1996; tax-free withdrawals were locked in by 2001. Ownership skews affluent (per GAO, holders earn ~3x non-holders).

**Coverdell ESA.** Launched 1997 as the "Education IRA," renamed in 2002 for Senator Paul Coverdell. Like a mini-529 ($2,000/yr cap) but usable for K–12 too. Largely obsolete now that 529s cover K–12 and have vastly higher limits — a good example of a redundant account that never got cleaned up.

### Health & disability

**HSA.** The only triple-tax-free account: deductible in, tax-free growth, tax-free out for medical. Created in 2003. The catch: you must be enrolled in a high-deductible health plan to contribute. $147B across 39M accounts — but only ~9% are invested; most are used as a debit card for current bills. The "stealth retirement account" strategy is real but operated by a sophisticated sliver.

**FSA.** The 1978 cafeteria-plan vehicle. Pre-tax money for medical or dependent-care costs, but the infamous "use it or lose it" rule: forfeit unspent funds at year-end (employers may allow a $660 carryover *or* a 2.5-month grace period, never both; dependent-care FSAs can't carry over at all). Employer-offered, lower limits (~$3,300 health FSA).

**HRA.** Employer-funded only — employees can't contribute. The employer reimburses qualified medical expenses tax-free. Several flavors (QSEHRA for small employers, ICHRA to fund individual-market premiums). Created via IRS guidance in 2002. Entirely employer-dependent.

**ABLE account.** A 529 for disability (§529A, 2014). Lets people with disabilities (onset before age 26) save without losing means-tested benefits like SSI/Medicaid. Post-tax, tax-free for disability expenses, ~$19,000/yr cap. Small but important for the population it serves.

### The newest arrival

**§530A "Trump Account."** Created in the 2025 OBBBA. A child savings account with a $1,000 federal seed for newborns (2025–2028 births), $5,000/yr contributions, post-tax in but — oddly — gains taxed as *ordinary income* on withdrawal (worse than a Roth). ~4 million signed up in the pilot. The proposal's Exhibit A for "Congress keeps shipping new accounts instead of fixing existing ones," and the direct inspiration for the USA's universal $1,000 birth seed.

## The pattern worth noticing

Read the "Created" column top to bottom: 1958, 1974, 1978, 1986, 1996, 1997, 2002, 2003, 2014, 2025. Nearly every account is a *separate act of Congress* adding a *new* vehicle — almost never simplifying or merging an old one. Each came with its own limits, eligibility tests, withdrawal rules, and acronym. That accretion *is* the problem the USA is trying to refactor away.
