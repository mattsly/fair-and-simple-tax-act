## Planned But Not Yet Stubbed

### Fiscal Infrastructure First
**Thesis:** Progressives keep proposing ambitious new programs on a foundation that can't support them. Before Medicare for All, before a Green New Deal, we need a tax system that's simple enough to actually work. That's not austerity. That's infrastructure.

**Key argument:** Warren's wealth tax and the capital gains reform proposed here aren't competing on the same objective. Warren's goal is to fund new programs; the goal here is to establish a simple, scalable tax system that closes the deficit and can be easily tuned to support new programs later. Before we debate what to fund, we need a system that can actually fund it reliably.

**Connection to Abundance:** This is the fiscal precondition argument — the same way Abundance argues you need to fix permitting and zoning before you can build housing, you need to fix the tax system before you can fund ambitious programs. Complexity in the foundation defeats everything built on top of it.

**Likely home:** Standalone Substack essay. Could also serve as the broader frame that makes the Warren wealth tax op-ed a case study rather than a one-off critique.

### Vibe Journalism (working title)
**Thesis:** Everyone is vibe coding, but I'm trying vibe journalism — using AI to research and write substantive policy pieces the same way non-programmers are using AI to ship apps. It's genuinely exciting. It's also still hard to do well and have real impact.

**Core argument:** AI democratized production but not distribution. Creation got cheaper; judgment, credibility, and audience-building didn't. The startup parallel is apt: have an idea, ship early and often, iterate — but distribution is always the real challenge. Matt has lived this multiple times (FutureMe, Amazon, Microsoft) so this isn't theoretical.

**Proposed structure:**
1. Hook: launching a startup and launching a policy idea feel surprisingly similar
2. Creation got cheaper (this is real) — faster iteration, better ideas, test arguments before publishing. Meta: this essay is itself vibe journalism, written with Claude. Don't hedge it.
3. Distribution is the same hard problem — an app on your laptop isn't a startup; a post in a Google Doc isn't journalism
4. The gotchas the hype skips — domain expertise still required to catch AI errors; credibility doesn't come from polish; the viber is accountable for the output
5. Landing: democratization is real but narrow — what got cheaper was production, not judgment, distribution, or credibility

**Connection to broader project:** This essay is the meta-frame for the whole Tax Refactor project. Could run as a standalone Substack piece or as a companion intro explaining the project's origins.

**Likely home:** Substack (early piece, could help with audience building). Personal/accessible tone, not policy-wonk.

### The Bribery Machine (working title)
**Thesis:** Both parties have turned the tax code into a constituency rewards system, just for different demographics. This isn't a departure from the "neutral, simple system" ideal — it's proof that a complex code with enough hidden levers will always be exploited. The only structural fix is a code simple enough that there's nothing to corrupt.

**Core examples:**
- Left: Warren's wealth tax (right diagnosis, wrong prescription — complexity in response to a real problem)
- Right: "No tax on tips" (nakedly a Nevada campaign play, not a policy), Nancy Mace's boat interest deduction (SC), carried interest survival despite promises
- Historical note: Republicans have always done this (Opportunity Zones, pass-through deduction, MID) — what's new is the nakedness of it. Previous distortions at least came with a policy rationale. These don't.

**Framing:** Both parties use the code differently — Dems to incentivize preferred behavior (EVs, clean energy, healthcare), Republicans increasingly to reward political demographics. Neither is the "neutral" system traditional GOP orthodoxy claimed to want.

**Connection to broader project:** "No tax on tips" is exhibit B alongside Warren's wealth tax — both are symptoms of a code complex enough to be gamed. A simple, transparent tax system is corruption-resistant by design.

### Who Really Owns "Free Markets"? (working title)
**Thesis:** Republicans have abandoned their own stated free-market principles while Democrats have failed to claim the actual Adam Smith tradition — which recognized externalities, opposed monopolies, and saw labor/time mobility as foundational.

**Two angles in tension (both worth developing):**
1. The rebrand angle: Democrats could own "pro-competition, pro-market" by framing unregulated monopolies (Amazon, Google, Meta, UnitedHealth) as anti-capitalist and anti-freedom. The "mobility of time and labor as capital" reframe is original — freedom to pursue your interests is at least as important as financial capital.
2. The hypocrisy angle (probably stronger politically): Don't introduce a new philosophy — strip Republicans of the brand they already have. No-tax-on-tips, Opportunity Zones, boat interest deductions aren't free markets. They're government picking winners. Make Republicans defend what they've actually built.

**Adam Smith note:** Smith was deeply worried about monopolies, merchant capture of policy, and worker exploitation. He's not the libertarian mascot conservatives think he is. This is a historical argument worth making but hard to win in political messaging.

**Open question:** Is this one essay or two? The hypocrisy argument and the "Democrats should claim free markets" argument are related but distinct.

**Connection to broader project:** The tax code is the most visible arena where both of these arguments play out. Can be tied back to the simplification thesis.
