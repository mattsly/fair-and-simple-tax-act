# FAQ: Why We're Getting the Government Out of Your Investment Decisions

*Part of the Fair and Simple Tax Act framework*

---

## Q1: What do you mean by "behavioral nudges" in the tax code?

The federal tax code is full of provisions that reward specific economic behaviors over others. Not because those behaviors are inherently better — but because a lobbyist, a trade association, or a political coalition got a carve-out written into law.

Right now, the code tells you:

- **What kind of business to start.** QSBS (Section 1202) excludes up to $15 million in capital gains — but only if you start a C-corp under $75M in assets, in qualifying industries, and hold for 5+ years. Start an LLC instead? Run a restaurant holding company? You're out of luck.
- **What kind of property to buy.** Section 1031 lets you defer capital gains on real estate indefinitely — but not on stocks, not on a small business, not on intellectual property. The code has decided real estate is special.
- **Where to invest.** Opportunity Zones give preferential capital gains treatment for investing in designated census tracts — 78% of OZ investment has gone to the top 5% of zones, mostly in areas that were already gentrifying.
- **Whether to sell your home.** Section 121 gives you $500K tax-free on a primary residence sale, which sounds great until you realize it locks people into homes they've outgrown and distorts housing supply.

Each of these provisions started with a reasonable-sounding goal. Together, they create a tax code that functions as a behavioral engineering system — one that mostly benefits people wealthy enough to hire advisors who know the rules exist.

## Q2: What would you eliminate, specifically?

All of the following special preferences would be repealed and replaced by one universal system:

| Current Provision | What It Does | Est. Federal Revenue Cost |
|---|---|---|
| **QSBS (Section 1202)** | Excludes up to $15M in gains on qualifying small business stock | ~$4.5B/yr (projected $44.5B over 10 yrs) |
| **Section 1031 (Like-Kind Exchanges)** | Indefinite deferral of gains on real estate swaps | ~$4-10B/yr (JCT estimates vary) |
| **Section 121 (Home Sale Exclusion)** | $250K/$500K tax-free gain on primary residence | ~$40-50B/yr |
| **Opportunity Zones** | Deferral + exclusion of gains invested in designated areas | ~$3.5B/yr |
| **Carried Interest** | Fund managers pay cap gains rates on labor income | ~$1.4B/yr |
| **Stepped-Up Basis at Death** | Heirs inherit assets at current value, erasing all gains | ~$58B/yr |

**Replaced by:** A progressive lifetime capital gains structure with a generous 0% bracket, applied universally to all asset types. No special forms. No qualifying tests. No sector restrictions.

## Q3: Why is this better? Those programs exist for good reasons.

Let's take them one at a time.

**QSBS: "We need to encourage entrepreneurship."**

Do we need to encourage it *this way*? QSBS requires you to form a C-corp (not an LLC or S-corp — the structures 95% of small businesses actually use), stay under $75M in assets, be in a qualifying industry, and hold for years. A Treasury analysis found that 70% of excluded QSBS dollars go to taxpayers with average income over $1 million making claims over $1 million. This isn't a small business program. It's a program for already-successful founders and their VC backers who happen to know the rules.

Under our proposal, *every* entrepreneur gets a 0% rate on their first tranche of lifetime gains — whether they started a software company, a landscaping business, a food truck, or a consulting practice. No special structure required. No qualifying tests. You build something, you sell it, the first chunk is tax-free. Period.

**Section 1031: "We need real estate market liquidity."**

Why does real estate get infinite tax deferral but nothing else does? If you sell stock to buy different stock, you pay tax. If you sell a small business to start another, you pay tax. But sell a strip mall and buy an apartment building? Tax-free forever — and if you hold until death, the gain disappears entirely via stepped-up basis.

The 1031 exchange industry — qualified intermediaries, specialist attorneys, dedicated software — exists solely because of this provision. That's not market liquidity. That's an artificial ecosystem created by a tax distortion.

**Opportunity Zones: "We need to invest in distressed communities."**

The evidence is damning. Research found that OZ investment is overwhelmingly concentrated in areas already attracting capital. 63% of OZ census tracts received zero investment. One percent of tracts got 42% of the money. The program has attracted over $100 billion in investment at significant cost to taxpayers, with little evidence it created economic activity that wouldn't have happened anyway.

If you want to invest in distressed communities, use direct spending — which can be targeted, measured, and adjusted. Don't launder the subsidy through a capital gains preference that primarily benefits wealthy investors.

**Section 121: "We need to protect middle-class homeowners."**

This is the hardest one politically. But ask yourself: why does the tax code treat your house differently from your retirement account, your small business, or your stock portfolio?

Under our proposal, the generous 0% lifetime bracket protects the vast majority of homeowners. A family selling their home with a $400K gain uses $400K of their lifetime allowance — and still has substantial room for other gains. The difference: they don't get a *special* break for housing that doesn't exist for any other form of wealth-building. The code stops telling you that owning a home is more virtuous than starting a business or investing in the stock market.

## Q4: Aren't some behavioral nudges good? What about the charitable deduction?

Fair challenge. Here's the principle:

**A tax preference is justified only when (a) the behavior it encourages produces a clear positive externality that the market would not adequately provide, and (b) the preference is the most efficient way to generate that externality.**

The charitable deduction passes this test. Private charitable giving produces public goods — medical research, disaster relief, education — that markets underprovide. And the deduction is broadly available to anyone who gives, regardless of what they give to or how they structure their donation (within reason).

QSBS fails this test. Starting a C-corp doesn't produce a positive externality that starting an LLC doesn't. The preference is available only to people who structure their business a specific way, in qualifying industries, under asset thresholds. It's not encouraging entrepreneurship broadly — it's subsidizing a narrow legal structure.

1031 exchanges fail this test. Swapping one investment property for another doesn't produce a positive externality. It produces a transaction — one that wouldn't need special tax treatment if we weren't overtaxing the sale of long-held assets in the first place. (Our duration-proration mechanism addresses the actual problem 1031s were designed to work around.)

Opportunity Zones fail this test. Place-based investment can produce positive externalities, but OZs are the *least* efficient way to generate them. Direct spending through programs like LIHTC, NMTC, or CDBG grants can be targeted to actual need. OZs can't.

The principle isn't "no nudges ever." It's: **the default should be neutrality, and any deviation from neutrality should carry a heavy burden of proof.**

## Q5: Won't eliminating QSBS hurt startups?

This is the objection we hear most from the tech and venture community. Let's look at it honestly.

**What QSBS actually does in practice:** It provides a tax windfall *after* a startup has already succeeded. By definition, you only claim QSBS when you sell stock at a large gain — meaning the company already worked. QSBS doesn't help you raise your seed round. It doesn't help you make payroll. It doesn't reduce the risk of starting a company. It's a reward for having already won.

**What drives startup formation:** Access to capital, talent density, market opportunity, and cultural tolerance for risk. No founder in the history of Silicon Valley said "I wasn't going to start this company, but then I heard about Section 1202." They start companies because they see an opportunity. The tax treatment of a hypothetical future exit is not in the top ten factors.

**What our proposal does instead:** The 0% lifetime bracket protects the first substantial tranche of gains for *every* entrepreneur — not just those who structured correctly. A founder who builds a company as an LLC, or whose company grows past $75M in assets (which is a *good thing*), or who operates in a non-qualifying industry gets the same treatment as the founder who checks every QSBS box.

**The uncomfortable truth:** QSBS disproportionately benefits founders and VCs who already have sophisticated tax counsel. The landscaper who builds a $3M business and sells it gets nothing from QSBS. Under our system, they get the same 0% bracket as the YC-backed founder.

## Q6: Won't eliminating 1031 exchanges crash the real estate market?

No. But the real estate lobby will tell you otherwise, so let's engage seriously.

**The lock-in argument:** Proponents say 1031s keep capital flowing by removing the "friction" of taxation on property sales. Without them, investors will hold properties longer, reducing transaction volume.

**The counter:** Our proposal addresses the legitimate concern behind 1031s — the bunching problem — through duration proration. If you hold a property for 20 years and sell it for a $2M gain, that gain gets spread across 20 years for rate purposes, not bunched into one tax year. This solves the actual economic problem without giving real estate a permanent preference over every other asset class.

**The dynastic wealth problem:** Combined with stepped-up basis, 1031 exchanges allow real estate portfolios to be passed down for generations with *zero* capital gains ever paid. The gain isn't deferred — it's eliminated. Our proposal (deemed realization at death) closes this loop permanently.

**The scale argument:** The JCT has estimated the tax expenditure for 1031 exchanges at roughly $4-10 billion per year. The real estate market in the United States involves trillions in annual transactions. The idea that a tax provision worth single-digit billions would "crash" a multi-trillion dollar market is not supported by evidence.

## Q7: Doesn't this amount to a massive tax increase?

It depends on who you are.

**If you're a middle-class family** with one home sale and a retirement portfolio: You almost certainly pay *less* under this system. The 0% lifetime bracket covers the vast majority of your lifetime capital gains. You no longer need to worry about whether your home sale qualifies for Section 121, or whether you held long enough, or whether you used it as a primary residence for 2 of the last 5 years. You just... sell your house.

**If you're a successful entrepreneur** with a $5M exit: You pay a moderate rate on gains above the 0% bracket. You might pay more than you would under current QSBS rules — but you also don't need to spend $20K on tax attorneys making sure your company qualifies. And if your company *didn't* qualify for QSBS (wrong structure, wrong industry, too much in assets), you pay dramatically less.

**If you're a serial real estate investor** rolling gains through 1031 exchanges indefinitely: Yes, you pay more. The code currently lets you defer — and ultimately eliminate — gains forever. That ends.

**If you're an ultra-high-net-worth individual** using buy-borrow-die to avoid ever realizing gains: Yes, you pay significantly more. That's the point.

The question isn't "does anyone pay more?" Of course some people do. The question is: **should the tax code be engineered to help specific groups avoid taxation based on which asset they hold, which structure they chose, or which advisor they hired?** We think the answer is no.

## Q8: How is this "radical simplicity" if you're adding lifetime tracking?

Fair pushback. Let's do the complexity math.

**What we're eliminating:**
- Section 1202 (QSBS): 8 qualifying tests, multiple holding period rules, industry restrictions, asset thresholds, special basis calculations, 1045 rollover rules
- Section 1031: 45-day identification windows, 180-day completion deadlines, qualified intermediary requirements, boot calculations, related-party rules, Form 8824
- Section 121: Primary residence tests, 2-of-5-year rules, partial exclusion calculations, divorce provisions, military exceptions
- Opportunity Zones: Fund certification, 90% asset tests, substantial improvement tests, 10-year holding requirements, Form 8996
- Carried interest: Complex allocation rules distinguishing investment from service income
- Stepped-up basis: Valuation disputes, date-of-death appraisals, alternate valuation dates

**What we're adding:**
- One lifetime running total, reported by brokerages (who already track cost basis) and maintained by the IRS

Modern brokerage platforms already track cost basis, acquisition dates, and cumulative gains. The infrastructure exists. What doesn't exist — and what we'd eliminate the need for — is the army of tax attorneys, qualified intermediaries, exchange accommodators, and OZ fund administrators whose entire business model depends on the current system's complexity.

## Q9: Isn't the government supposed to use tax policy to encourage good behavior?

This is the philosophical heart of the debate.

We'd argue the tax code's primary job is to **raise revenue fairly and efficiently** — not to be a shadow regulatory system that picks economic winners and losers. When Congress wants to encourage homeownership, clean energy, or small business formation, there are better tools: direct spending, grants, loan guarantees, regulatory reform. These tools are transparent, measurable, and can be adjusted without rewriting the tax code.

Tax preferences are the worst way to deliver subsidies because they're invisible (most taxpayers don't know they exist), regressive (they're worth more to people in higher brackets), and durable (once created, they're nearly impossible to repeal because they create constituencies that lobby for their preservation).

The 1031 exchange is a perfect example. It was created in 1921. A century later, it survives not because anyone has proven it serves the public interest, but because an entire industry — qualified intermediaries, specialist attorneys, trade associations — depends on its existence and lobbies ferociously for its preservation.

**Our position:** The default setting of the tax code should be *neutrality*. Treat all gains the same. Treat all assets the same. Treat all industries the same. If Congress wants to subsidize something, do it on-budget where voters can see it and evaluate it. Don't hide it in the tax code where only the well-advised benefit.

## Q10: Canada has a lifetime capital gains system and it's gotten more complex over time. What makes you think yours won't?

This is the best objection anyone can make, and we take it seriously.

Canada's Lifetime Capital Gains Exemption (LCGE) started relatively clean in 1972 and has accumulated layers of complexity over 50 years — qualifying tests, industry carve-outs, the new Canadian Entrepreneurs' Incentive with its own eligibility criteria and phase-in schedules. CPA Canada itself has questioned whether "creating complex tax benefits to offset other complex tax rules helps or hinders the tax system."

Three design choices in our proposal are specifically intended to resist this trajectory:

1. **Universality.** Canada's LCGE applies only to qualified small business shares and farm/fishing property. That narrow scope *invites* lobbying for expansion to other categories, which creates new boundaries, which creates new complexity. Our system applies to all capital gains from all sources. There are no qualifying tests because there are no categories. The dairy farmer, the app developer, and the day trader all face the same rules.

2. **Structural simplicity.** Our mechanism is a single running total with progressive brackets. There's nothing to "carve out" because there are no special provisions to carve out *from*. Canada's complexity grew because each exception created a boundary that needed policing. We have no exceptions, so there are no boundaries.

3. **The three-repeal anchor.** By simultaneously abolishing the estate tax, AMT, and NIIT, we create a political constituency (primarily Republican) invested in the new system's *survival*. Rolling back to the old system would mean re-imposing three taxes the repeal coalition fought to eliminate. This makes complexity creep harder because any "improvement" that threatens the system's simplicity risks unraveling the entire deal.

Will lobbying pressure exist? Absolutely. Real estate will want its 1031 back. VCs will want their QSBS back. The question is whether the system's architecture makes it harder to grant those requests without breaking the whole structure. We believe it does.

But we're not naive. The honest answer is: **every tax system trends toward complexity over time.** The goal isn't perfection — it's raising the cost of complexity so that each new exception has to clear a higher bar. A system with zero exceptions is harder to degrade than a system that already has fifty.
