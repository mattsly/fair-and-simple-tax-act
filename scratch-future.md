

## The QSBS Myth

Here is where I expect the loudest objection, and it will come from Silicon Valley.

The Qualified Small Business Stock exclusion (Section 1202 of the tax code) allows up to $10 million in gains from certain startup stock to be excluded from federal capital gains tax entirely. The venture capital industry treats QSBS as sacred: the foundation of the startup ecosystem, the reason Google, NVIDIA, and OpenAI are American companies.

The historical timeline does not support this narrative.

QSBS was enacted in 1993 at a 50% exclusion. It rose to 75% in 2009 and didn't reach 100% until September 2010. It wasn't made permanent until the PATH Act of 2015. NVIDIA was founded in April 1993, the same year QSBS was enacted, before it could have influenced anything. Google IPO'd in August 2004, when the exclusion was 50%. Apple, Microsoft, Amazon, and Intel were all built in a world with no QSBS at all.

The American startup ecosystem was created by deep capital markets, world-class research universities, immigration policy, and rule of law. QSBS is a windfall layered on top of an ecosystem that was already the envy of the world. Attributing the ecosystem to a tax provision that reached its current form in 2010 requires believing that a tax break retroactively explains companies founded decades earlier.

International evidence confirms this. Israel has roughly one startup per 1,400 people, the highest density globally and arguably more startup-dense per capita than the United States. Israel has no equivalent of QSBS. The UK's Enterprise Investment Scheme and Seed Enterprise Investment Scheme are narrower, more targeted, and capped at much lower levels. Sweden, Canada, and Germany all produce thriving startup sectors without blanket exclusions for startup stock.

And who actually benefits from QSBS? Not first-time founders with modest exits; they're protected by this framework's $2.5 million exemption regardless. The largest beneficiaries are serial entrepreneurs and venture capital partners who are already wealthy. Peter Thiel used QSBS-eligible PayPal shares to build a $5 billion tax-free Roth IRA, as reported by ProPublica. The risk premium for someone deploying capital from an existing $50 million net worth into their fourth company is negligible. They would make that investment at a 7% effective tax rate. They would make it at 15%. The zero-tax QSBS exclusion is not what motivates the bet.

The honest concession: QSBS elimination does marginally increase the tax cost for early-stage employees at pre-IPO startups. A senior engineer who joins a Series A company, holds for five-plus years, and exits with $3 million in gains currently pays zero. Under this framework, they'd pay a small amount on the $500,000 above the exemption, roughly 2-3% effective. That's real. But the trade is a universal, simple exemption that covers every type of risk-taker, not just those who happened to join C-corps that clear arbitrary technical hurdles.

## The Transition Is Not the Hard Part

Canada faced a harder version of this problem in 1972, going from zero capital gains tax to a full system. It chose a "Valuation Day" approach: all assets deemed acquired at fair market value on a fixed date. Everything before that was wiped clean. The system has worked for over fifty years. This framework uses the same approach. The lifetime counter starts at zero on the date of enactment. Only post-enactment gains count. Will some investors sell beforehand to lock in current rates? Yes, and the government wins anyway: under the old system with stepped-up basis, many of those gains would never have been taxed at all. Will it crash the market? No. Pre-enactment harvesting is overwhelmingly round-trip (sell to reset basis, immediately rebuy), and a nine-to-twelve-month transition window allows orderly adjustment.



Executive Summary: The Capital Gains Refactor
Goal: Restore the U.S. federal budget to a sustainable 3% of GDP through simplicity, fairness, and the elimination of "wealth-hiding" loopholes.

The Problem: A Broken Interface

The current tax code is a "design failure." It rewards those who can afford sophisticated tax shielding while penalizing those who build wealth through transparent, traditional means. Strategies like "Buy, Borrow, Die" allow the ultra-wealthy to live on untaxed appreciation indefinitely, while the middle class faces complex rules for home sales and retirement.

The Solution: Five Simple Rules

By moving to a Lifetime Cumulative Ledger, we can replace thousands of pages of tax code with a system that fits on a single page:

The $1.5M Personal Shield: Your first $1.5M in lifetime capital gains (homes, stocks, business exits) is 0% tax. This is indexed to inflation, ensuring your "nest egg" is protected from bracket creep.

The Fair-Share Phase-Out: Between $1.5M and $7.5M in lifetime gains, the rate slides up to your ordinary income rate.

Realization at the Finish Line: We close the "Step-up at Death" and "Borrowing" loopholes. Selling, dying, or borrowing against assets triggers a realization event. No more living on tax-free loans.

Inflation Indexing: You are only taxed on real wealth. Your cost basis is adjusted by the CPI, so you aren't penalized for the eroding value of the dollar.

Roth Reform: We cap Roth IRA balances at $5M to prevent "Mega-Roths" while removing income limits to make them accessible to more workers.

The Fiscal Impact: Deficit Reduction
Based on 2026 economic projections, this "Refactor" isn't just simpler—it's a massive revenue engine for the Treasury.

Annual Revenue Projections (Net Gain: ~$220B)

+$100B from eliminating "Stepped-Up Basis" at death.

+$75B from taxing asset-backed loans (Closing "Buy, Borrow, Die").

+$95B from higher rates on the top 0.5% of earners (the $7.5M+ tier).

-$50B (Cost) to provide the $1.5M "Universal Exemption" to all citizens.

The 3% GDP Goal

When paired with 15% discretionary cost-cutting and entitlement modernization, this proposal provides the critical ~25% of the total revenue needed to bring the federal deficit down from its current 5.8% to a healthy 3.1% of GDP.

The "Bottom Line" for the Voter

For 95% of Americans: You will likely never pay capital gains tax again. Your home, your modest stock portfolio, and your small business exit are fully shielded by the $1.5M lifetime exemption.

For the Ultra-Wealthy: You pay the same rates as the people who work for you. No more "hiding" wealth in loans or passing it untaxed to heirs.
