# Dear Tech Bros: Stop Whining about Wealth Taxes and "Get in the Arena"

A few weeks ago, I [wrote a piece](https://taxrefactor.substack.com/p/senator-warren-is-right-about-the) arguing that  Elizabeth Warren has the right diagnosis (billionaires pay lower effective rates than teachers, "buy/borrow/die" is indefensible) but the wrong prescription: a wealth tax is a new system bolted onto a 75,000-page mess that already doesn't work. Right problem, wrong solution.

Today I want to point a similar critique in a different direction - the indignance that "tech bros" have shown towards various efforts to tax extreme wealth.

Take, for example, the [All-In Podcast](https://www.allinpodcast.co/). Many years ago, when it was first launched, All In used to be my favorite listen. I appreciated the raw, unfiltered, often contrarian takes on what was happening in the technology markets. Four smart and experienced people, different ideologies, stress-testing each other in public. It was a good product.

But somewhere along the way the "first principles" they espoused became "me-first principles." Chamath and Sacks went all-in on Trump and Trump-era cronyism, despite [having said after January 6](https://www.thebulwark.com/p/trumps-tech-bro-fanboys-wanted-him) that he should be barred from public office. The show veered hard into politics, and the obvious hypocrisy and political tone-deafness of four very rich guys became overwhelming. So I stopped listening.

Lately, I've caught wind of their latest target of indignation: wealth tax policy. If Senator Warren is offering the wrong answer to extreme wealth inequality, the All-In guys are offering *no* answer. And refusing to propose anything while complaining about everything is, in some ways, the more frustrating posture. It abdicates the chance to actually shape the outcome.

## The whining problem

Efforts to curb extreme wealth disparity are gaining momentum. As "friend-of-the-pod" [Ray Dalio has repeatedly pointed out](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/big-cycles-over-last-500-years-ray-dalio/), it never ends well.

[Massachusetts passed a millionaire's tax in 2022](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/4-surtax-on-taxable-income-over-1000000). [Mamdani won in New York on a platform focused on taxing the rich](https://www.nytimes.com/article/who-is-zohran-mamdani.html) running on (among other things) a pied-à-terre tax and an income surcharge on the rich. [Seattle has its JumpStart payroll tax](https://www.seattle.gov/business-license-tax/payroll-expense-tax) on high earners at large companies, raising over $250M annually (I think Seattle also enacted a one time wealth tax which drove out Howard Schulz?). California is now moving toward [a 5% wealth tax on net worth over $50M](https://www.cnbc.com/2024/01/29/california-wealth-tax-bill.html).

But the All-In responses have ranged from:
1. You'll scare away capital allocators aka "job creators"
2. I trust Elon to deploy capital more than the government
3. Move to Austin, it's great.
4. Mamdani is a socialist.
5. You can't tax unrealized gains!
6. All we need to do is cut waste, fraud, and abuse.
7. Just unleash AI and the deficit takes care of itself.

That is not a "first principles" policy response. It is just a scattershot way of complaining devoid of self-awareness. 

Friedberg has complained about the deficit, but seems to think that growth and cost cutting are the only levers available and has not once, to my knowledge, suggested any ideas for growing revenue.

You can maybe dismiss any one of these new state level tax policies as a local fluke. You cannot dismiss all of them. There is a political groundswell gaining momentum around the issue of extreme wealth disparity, as it has many times before in history. And when the efforts to tax wealth go federal (and I think it will), the pied-à-terre in Miami doesn't help.
So rather than just complaining about what are indeed hacky, bespoke, one-off tax policies, how about a) recognizing hte social risk of wealth disparity and b) actually proposing some viable, systems-oriented "first principles" alternatives?

## The wonky section (skip if you want, but you shouldn't)

The All-In response to taxing wealth tends to be "we earned it, leave us alone, and let us cook so we can create more jobs for the rest of you. Go DOGE away all of the fraud." That response leaves out some load-bearing facts.

**1. You did not build this alone.** The internet you got rich on came out of [DARPA-funded research from the 1960s onward](https://nap.nationalacademies.org/catalog/6323/funding-a-revolution-government-support-for-computing-research), with tens of billions of federal R&D dollars over decades. The semiconductor industry was built on Bell Labs (effectively a regulated public utility), DOD procurement, federally-funded basic research, and most recently the [$52B CHIPS Act](https://www.commerce.gov/news/blog/2022/09/one-year-chips-and-science-act). Your educated workforce came out of public universities and federally-subsidized student loans. Your contracts get enforced by federal courts. Your products ship on public roads. Your IPO got cleared by the SEC. None of this is socialism. It is just true. The point is not "you owe us." The point is that the system that made you rich is a system, and a system needs maintenance. (More reading: Mariana Mazzucato's [*The Entrepreneurial State*](https://marianamazzucato.com/books/the-entrepreneurial-state/).)

**2. Luck did more than you think.** I am not saying you are not talented or hard-working or deserve to be incredibly wealthy. But I am saying that talent and hard work are necessary but nowhere close to sufficient ingredients in your financial success. The gap between "talented" and "lottery winner" is mostly random. A society that taxes luck-driven windfalls more than effort-driven income is, if anything, *more* meritocratic than the current system. Not less. (More reading: a [2018 paper by Pluchino, Biondo, and Rapisarda](https://arxiv.org/abs/1802.07068) in *Advances in Complex Systems* simulates 1,000 agents with normally-distributed talent and randomly-distributed luck. The most successful agent had a talent score of 0.61. The mean was 0.6. The most talented agent, at 0.89, ended up below average in success. The Pareto-distributed wealth tail is almost entirely luck-driven.)

**3. Wealth begets wealth.** Once you are already rich, your next venture has a 10x advantage over the kid in the dorm room with the same idea. You take less risk. You stay rich if it fails. You can self-fund. You can absorb losses. You can wait out bad markets. You have the network. The current tax code amplifies this advantage (step-up basis, [QSBS](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/q/qsbs-qualified-small-business-stock.asp) games, founder stock loopholes, the [buy/borrow/die playbook](https://www.propublica.org/article/the-secret-irs-files-trove-of-never-before-seen-records-reveal-how-the-wealthiest-avoid-income-tax), perpetually lower capital gains rates). If you actually want a meritocracy of ideas, advocate for one.

**4. Pay attention to history.** As noted, Ray Dalio wrote *the* book on this: [*Principles for Dealing with the Changing World Order*](https://www.principles.com/the-changing-world-order/). Extreme wealth concentration ends the same way every time, and that way is bad. French Revolution. Russian Revolution. Weimar collapse. The Great Depression. This is not a leftist talking point. This is pattern recognition from the guy you keep quoting.

## And now, the AI part

Your investment thesis (and you say it on every other episode) is that AI will unleash an unprecedented period of economic growth and we can grow our way out of the deficit. 

If you are right, then:

- Labor market disruption hits faster than any retraining or social safety net can absorb. Calacanis himself has talked about young workers (20–24) seeing [unemployment spikes from AI replacement](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/generative-ai-the-american-worker-and-the-future-of-work/). Right now. Pre-hockey-stick.
- Wealth concentration accelerates dramatically. AI markets concentrate even more aggressively than software markets did, because frontier model development is a multi-billion-dollar fixed cost only a handful of companies can absorb. The companies that own the models and the chips capture an obscene fraction of global productivity gains. Meanwhile those who produced the content that is being used to train the models are not being compensated at all (? add a link here?)
- The political response to an increasing gap between the uber-wealthy and everyone else will be aggressive, populist, and not designed by people who understand capital formation. (? I think we need some more here...links or references etc...)

AI's public reputation is already in the toilet. You are about to ride a productivity boom into a country that already does not trust the people building it. You cannot bolt a 21st-century safety net onto a tax system that requires a 75,000-page reference manual to operate. The thing that makes UBI scary to libertarians is not UBI itself, it is the idea of layering it on top of EITC, SNAP, TANF, housing vouchers, and 80 different phase-outs. Strip the existing mess down to studs first. Then we can argue about what to build. ## Five things you should be loudly for
Here are several centrist ideas that a true AI accelerationist should support:
**1. Portable benefits.** Healthcare and retirement decoupled from employer. Workers carry them across jobs. Better for the gig economy, better for entrepreneurship, better for AI-driven labor disruption. Why is this not a libertarian-coded position already? It should be. Friedberg, this one is right in your wheelhouse. Advocate for it.

**2. A radically simpler tax code.** No itemization. No phase-outs. Close the loopholes. Kill the alphabet soup of credits and deductions. Automate everything. [Compliance costs in the U.S. run $536B per year](https://www.ntu.org/foundation/detail/who-pays-for-tax-complexity). That is pure deadweight. A multi-billion-dollar tax prep industry exists because the code is broken. Imagine a tax return that fits on an index card. (I have a [draft](https://www.mattsly.com/fair-and-simple-tax-act).)

**3. FICA reform.** The wage cap is currently $176,100. Anyone earning above that pays exactly zero additional Social Security tax on the next dollar. So a worker making $80K pays FICA on every dollar of wages; a hedge fund manager pays it on a tiny fraction of theirs. This is a regressive tax pretending to be a flat one. Lift the cap. Pair it with progressive benefit adjustments at the top so it is not a pure transfer. (Romney proposed something close to this. So have [several Senate Democrats](https://www.whitehouse.senate.gov/news/release/whitehouse-larson-introduce-medicare-and-social-security-fair-share-act/). The bipartisan version is right there for the taking.)

**4. Universal Savings Accounts.** Collapse the alphabet soup (401k, 403b, IRA, Roth, HSA, FSA, 529, Coverdell, ABLE, SEP, SIMPLE) into one Roth-style account everyone has from birth. Same tax treatment. Same contribution limits. Portable. Simple. This should be a libertarian fever dream and instead nobody is championing it.

**5. Close buy/borrow/die.** Death is a realization event. Step-up basis ends. A lifetime capital gains framework with a meaningful exemption (so we are not chasing middle-class retirees) and a clean transition (no retroactive tax on gains accrued before the change). This is the *alternative* to a wealth tax. It taxes realized gains instead of trying to value an illiquid asset every year, and it runs on infrastructure the IRS already operates. (?  why should they support this? just b/c it's so obviously unfair and will otherwise be closed in a hacky way?)

6. Some kind of UBI / "AI dividend" direct payment that can replace all the handout and bureacrazy programs?

Quick note here: [Tucker Carlson](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/05/02/magazine/tucker-carlson-interview.html), who like Dalio has been on the show and is a "Friend of the Pod," has been openly questioning why capital gets taxed at half the rate of labor. He raised it again in his recent NYT *Daily* interview, talking about "the true hoarding of capital by a tiny group of people." When the populist right starts asking that question, the political coalition defending the current treatment is already gone. You just haven't noticed yet.

(Yes, closing buy/borrow/die would cost some of you money. Be honest about it. The alternative is not "no change." The alternative is a wealth tax designed by people who do not care about capital formation. Pick your poison.)

## Some people are actually getting in the arena

Before I list what you should be advocating for, let me name a few extremely wealthy people who have, to use Chamath's phrase, gotten in the arena.

In [December 2025, Mitt Romney wrote a New York Times op-ed](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/12/19/opinion/romney-tax-the-rich.html) titled, in essence, "tax me more, I was wrong before." He called the loopholes "tax caverns." He named carried interest, the FICA cap, real estate depreciation, and the stepped-up basis on inheritances over $100M as specific things to fix. He used Elon Musk as an example of how the current system breaks. He is a former Republican presidential nominee and former Bain Capital founder, and he put his name on a concrete list. The [WSJ editorial board attacked him for it](https://thehill.com/homenews/media/5659642-wsj-romney-tax-rich-criticism/). That is what taking a real position looks like.

Mark Cuban, who paid $275.9M in taxes in 2023 and was [proudly public about it](https://www.cnbc.com/2024/04/15/how-much-mark-cuban-pays-in-taxes.html), has been actively advising campaigns on capital gains policy. He talked Harris down from Biden's higher cap gains proposal because he can articulate why higher rates discourage investment in early-stage, riskier companies (especially those started by women, people of color, and recent grads). He disagrees with Warren on unrealized gains. He thinks billionaires should pay more under the current realization-based system. That is a coherent position with specifics. You can disagree with it. It is in the arena.

Bill Gates and Warren Buffett have been [publicly arguing for years](https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2024ltr.pdf) that people like them should pay more. Buffett's Berkshire Hathaway paid a record $26.8B in federal taxes in 2024. The "Buffett Rule" was a specific proposal with a number attached.

You do not have to agree with any of these people. But they made proposals. They put their names on something concrete. They got attacked by their own side and held their position. *That* is what "getting in the arena" looks like.

Get in the arena, guys.
