A wealth tax is a new system bolted onto a 75,000-page mess that already doesn't work. Right problem, wrong solution.

((note - don't want to say "my side" - I'm not sure I have a side...) And now it's their turn. Because if I'm going to call out Warren for offering the wrong answer, I have to call out the All-In guys for offering *no* answer. And right now, "lower taxes and move to Austin" is not an answer. ## The wonky section (skip if you want, but you shouldn't)

The All-In response to the idea of taxing wealth tends to be "we earned it, leave us alone and let us cook and create more jobs for the rest of you." And that response leaves out some load-bearing facts.

Here are some counterpoints to that perspective:

**1. You did not build this alone.** The internet you got rich on was DARPA (? say more? how much fed funding went into this?). The semiconductor industry was Bell Labs and federal R&D (? how much). Your educated workforce came out of universities supported with public dollars. Your contracts get enforced by federal courts. Your products ship on public roads and your data transferred through publicly regulated networking infrastructure. Your IPO got cleared by the SEC. The point is not "you owe us." The point is "the system that made you rich is a system, and a system needs maintenance." (More reading: Mariana Mazzucato's *The Entrepreneurial State*.)

**2. Luck did more than you think.** I'm not saying you're not talented or hard working or that you don't deserve to be incredibly wealthy. But I am saying that talent and hard work is necessary but nowhere close to sufficient path to success. The gap between talent and "lottery winner" is highly - even mostly - random. A society that taxes luck-driven windfalls more than effort-driven income is, if anything, *more* meritocratic than the current system. Not less. (More reading: A 2018 paper by Pluchino, Biondo, and Rapisarda published in *Advances in Complex Systems* that simulates 1,000 agents with normally-distributed talent and randomly-distributed luck. The finding: the most successful agent had a talent score of 0.61. The mean was 0.6. The most talented agent (0.89) ended up below average in success. The Pareto-distributed wealth tail is almost entirely luck-driven.)

**3. Wealth begets wealth.** Once you're already rich, your next venture has a 10x advantage over the kid in the dorm room with the same idea. You are taking less risk. You are still rich if it fails. You can self-fund. You can absorb losses. You can wait out bad markets. You have the network. The current tax code amplifies this advantage (step-up basis, QSBS games, founder stock loopholes, the buy/borrow/die playbook, perpertually lower capital gains rates). If you actually want a meritocracy of ideas, then advocate for one.

**4. Pay attention to history.** As noted a above, Ray Dalio wrote *the* book on this. *Principles for Dealing with the Changing World Order*. Extreme wealth concentration ends the same way every time, and that way is bad. French Revolution. Russian Revolution. Weimar collapse. The Great Depression. This is not a leftist talking point. This is pattern recognition from the guy you keep quoting.

## And now, the AI part

Your investment thesis (and you say it on every other episode) is that AI will compress decades of change into years and that we can grow our way out of a deficit. Cool. Let's take that seriously for a minute.

If you're right, then:

- Labor market disruption hits faster than any retraining or social safety net can absorb. Calacanis himself has talked about young workers (20–24) seeing unemployment spikes from AI replacement. Right now. Pre-hockey-stick.
- Wealth concentration continues accelerates dramatically with (?? the global winners dynamic...). The companies that own the models and the chips capture an obscene fraction of global productivity gains.
- The political response to all of this will be aggressive, populist, and not designed by people who understand capital formation.

As you've noted, AI's public reputation is already in the toilet. Polling consistently shows Americans more worried than excited about AI, especially on jobs. (? can we add a link) You are about to ride a productivity boom into a country that already doesn't trust the people building it. If the productivity gains visibly accrue to the same fifty people while everyone else watches their job get automated, you don't get a wealth tax. You get something worse.

So if you actually believe your own thesis, you should be the *loudest* voices in America for a tax code that has the dexterity necessary to adapt to a new world of AI.

You cannot bolt a 21st-century safety net onto a tax system that requires a 75,000-page reference manual to operate. The thing that makes UBI scary to libertarians is not UBI itself, it's the idea of layering it on top of EITC and SNAP and TANF and housing vouchers and 80 different phase-outs. Strip the existing mess down to studs first. Then we can argue about what to build.

## Five things you should be loudly for

Here is a recommended starter pack. Pick one. Make it your thing.

**1. Portable benefits.** Healthcare and retirement decoupled from employer. Workers carry them across jobs. Better for the gig economy, better for entrepreneurship, better for AI-driven labor disruption. Why is this not a libertarian-coded position already? It should be. Friedberg, this one is right in your wheelhouse. Advocate for it.

**2. A radically simpler tax code.** No itemization. No phase-outs. Close the loopholes. Kill the alphabet soup of credits and deductions. Automate everything. Compliance costs in the U.S. run $536 billion per year. That is pure deadweight. A multi-billion-dollar tax prep industry exists because the code is broken. Imagine a tax return that fits on an index card. (I have a draft.)

**3. FICA reform.** The wage cap is currently $176,100. Anyone earning above that pays exactly zero additional Social Security tax on the next dollar. So a worker making $80K pays FICA on every dollar; a hedge fund manager pays it on a tiny fraction of theirs. This is a regressive tax pretending to be a flat one. Lift the cap. Pair it with progressive benefit adjustments at the top so it's not a pure transfer. This makes the tax base match the actual wage base.

**4. Universal Savings Accounts.** Collapse the alphabet soup (401k, 403b, IRA, Roth, HSA, FSA, 529, Coverdell, ABLE, SEP, SIMPLE) into one Roth-style account everyone has from birth. Same tax treatment. Same contribution limits. Portable. Simple. This should be a libertarian fever dream and instead nobody is championing it.

**5. Close buy/borrow/die.** Death is a realization event. Step-up basis ends. A lifetime capital gains framework with a meaningful exemption (so we're not chasing middle-class retirees) and a clean transition (no retroactive tax on gains accrued before the change). This is the *alternative* to a wealth tax. It taxes realized gains instead of trying to value an illiquid asset every year, and it runs on infrastructure the IRS already operates.

Quick note here: even Tucker Carlson, who you've had on the show, has been openly questioning why capital gets taxed at half the rate of labor. He raised it again in his recent NYT *Daily* interview, talking about "the true hoarding of capital by a tiny group of people." When the populist right starts asking that question, the political coalition defending the current treatment is already gone. You just haven't noticed yet.

(Yes, closing buy/borrow/die would cost some of you money. Be honest about it. The alternative is not "no change." The alternative is a wealth tax designed by people who don't care about capital formation. Pick your poison.)
