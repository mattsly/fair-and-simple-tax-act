# One Account to Rule Them All

### Replacing 15+ savings vehicles with one universal account

*By Matt Sly*

---

> **Status: Working draft.** This is a companion proposal to [The Tax Refactor](./index.md). Feedback welcome at me@mattsly.com.

---

## The Problem

The federal government currently incentivizes saving through at least 15 distinct account types: Traditional 401(k), Roth 401(k), Traditional IRA, Roth IRA, SEP IRA, SIMPLE IRA, 529 College Savings Plans, Coverdell Education Savings Accounts, ABLE Accounts, Health Savings Accounts (HSAs), Flexible Spending Accounts (FSAs), Health Reimbursement Arrangements (HRAs), 457(b) Deferred Compensation Plans, Thrift Savings Plans, and employer-sponsored pension plans.

Each has its own contribution limits, income limits, eligibility rules, withdrawal penalties, required minimum distributions (or lack thereof), employer matching rules, and tax treatment. A financially literate person with a good accountant can navigate these to maximize tax-free growth. A normal person cannot. The complexity itself is a regressive tax on financial literacy.

And the rules aren't just numerous, they're actively user-hostile. Your FSA is "use it or lose it": spend it by December 31 or forfeit it, unless your employer offers a carryover (capped at $660) or a 2.5-month grace period, but never both, and dependent-care FSAs can't roll over at all. Traditional IRAs and 401(k)s force you to start draining the account at 73 whether you need the money or not (the Required Minimum Distribution). Pull money out before 59½ and you eat a 10% penalty on top of the tax. Earn too much and you're locked out of contributing to a Roth IRA, unless you perform the "backdoor Roth," a real maneuver with a real name that exists only because the income limit exists, so the well-advised step around it and everyone else just loses access. None of this protects anyone. It's sediment.

The result: higher-income households with access to financial advisors optimize across multiple account types while lower-income households underutilize even the simplest options. [Nearly half of American workers have no access to a workplace retirement plan](https://www.bls.gov/ncs/ebs/benefits/2023/employee-benefits-in-the-united-states-march-2023.pdf), and of those who do, participation rates vary wildly based on plan complexity and employer match design.

## The Proposal: Universal Savings Account (USA)

One account. One set of rules. Every American.

**How it works:**

- **After-tax contributiosn, Tax-free growth.** No taxes on dividends, interest, or capital gains inside the account. The account is modeled after Roth IRAs.
- **Qualified Withdrawls**: higher education, first time home purchase, retirement after age 59.5
- **Contributions withdrawable anytime.** No penalty, no waiting period. Your contributions are your money.
- **$1,000 government seed at birth.** Every American starts with a funded account. This is the most progressive wealth-building policy available: it gives every child a compounding head start regardless of family income.
- **$30,000 annual contribution cap** (after-tax, indexed to CPI). One cap, every source. Whether the dollars come from you, an employer, or a generous grandparent, they all count against the same $30,000. There is no separate, higher employer limit the way 401(k)s work today (where you can put in $23,500 but the combined employee-plus-employer limit runs to $70,000). One number, no gaming.
- **$5 million balance cap (indexed to CPI).** Prevents the account from becoming an unlimited tax shelter (closes the Thiel Loophole from day one). Once you hit the cap, you stop contributing, but the balance keeps growing tax-free. The cap inflation-adjusts in parallel with the Lifetime Gains exemption and the income tax brackets, so the wrapper keeps its real value across decades. This is the *only* balance cap in the system: it absorbs the separate $5M Roth cap that Rule 5 of the [Lifetime Gains Framework](./lifetime-gains-essay.md) originally proposed. One cap, one place.
- **Qualified medical withdrawals: tax-free and penalty-free at any age.** This consolidates the HSA benefit into the same account, eliminating a separate vehicle with its own rules. "Qualified" means the existing IRS definition (Section 213(d)), the same expense list HSAs use today, ported wholesale rather than reinvented. (You can't double-dip: a dollar of medical spending you cover tax-free from your USA doesn't also count toward the Phase 2 medical credit. One dollar, one tax break. More on that below.)
- **Unqualified Withdrawls before age 60:** Taxed as capital gains, using the [Lifetime Gains Framework](./lifetime-gains-essay.md) exemption and sliding scale. Early withdrawal of gains is possible but not tax-free. (? no penalty? just pay taxes I guess that's right...)
- **Gains after age 60:** Tax-free up to $2.5 million in total withdrawals (indexed). Gains above $2.5 million are taxed as capital gains under the Lifetime Gains Framework.
- **No required minimum distributions.** You are never forced to withdraw.
(? need to describe what happens at death too)

**What it replaces:** 401(k)s (Traditional and Roth), IRAs (Traditional and Roth), SEP IRAs, SIMPLE IRAs, 529 plans, Coverdell ESAs, ABLE accounts, HSAs, FSAs, HRAs, and 457(b) plans.

## Why One Account?
(? some more ideas - once a year "open [sic] enrollment" is so annoying... and can we add some meat to this section with data and analysis and numbers? these are strong arguments that feel right, but they feel speculative without more evidence)

Complex savings systems disproportionately benefit higher-income households with advisors. One universal account increases participation, reduces administrative costs, and makes public subsidies for saving transparent and fair.

The current system forces people to make decisions they are not equipped to make: Traditional vs. Roth? IRA vs. 401(k)? Should I max my HSA first? What's my MAGI for backdoor Roth eligibility? Every one of these questions is an opportunity for the well-advised to optimize and the under-advised to lose.

A single account with simple rules eliminates these decisions. You save. It grows tax-free. You access it when you need it (contributions anytime, medical expenses anytime, everything else after 60). That's it.

Even early withdrawals of gains benefit from years of tax-free compounding. The structure rewards patience without punishing flexibility.

## Other Countries Already Do a Simpler Version

This isn't a thought experiment. The building blocks are running abroad right now.

The UK has the Individual Savings Account (ISA): you can put in £20,000 a year, and every pound of interest, dividends, and capital gains inside it is tax-free, permanently, with nothing to report on your tax return. Britons don't agonize over the tax treatment of their savings the way Americans do, because for most people it's one wrapper and one number.

Canada has the Tax-Free Savings Account (TFSA): about C$7,000 a year, tax-free growth, and one genuinely elegant feature the USA should steal outright. When you withdraw money, your contribution room comes back the next year. Take out $10,000 for an emergency in 2026 and in 2027 you can put it back on top of your normal room. The account doesn't punish you for using it.

And the $1,000 birth seed? The UK did that too. The Child Trust Fund, launched in 2005, dropped a £250 voucher into an account for every newborn (£500 for low-income families), with another £250 at age 7. Every British child born for most of a decade started life with a funded, compounding account.

Here's the honest part. None of these countries has gone all the way to a single account. The UK has at least five flavors of ISA, and from 2027 it's cutting the cash ISA limit to £12,000 for under-65s, exactly the kind of barnacle this project exists to scrape off. Canada runs the TFSA alongside an RRSP, an RESP, and an FHSA. The UK eventually replaced the Child Trust Fund with the Junior ISA, trading one clean idea for a slightly more complicated one. They've proven the pieces work. Nobody has assembled them into one account and kept it simple. That's the part the USA does.

## What Happens to the Old Accounts?

Roughly [$40 trillion sits in existing U.S. retirement (? not just retirement - what about HSA etc?) accounts](https://www.ici.org/statistical-report/ret_23_q4). We don't have to move it, and we shouldn't pay people a fortune to move it. Two mechanisms, in order of importance.

**First, deprecate.** On enactment, the legacy account types close to new money. No new 401(k)s, IRAs, 529s, HSAs, or the rest, and no new contributions to the ones that already exist. Every dollar of new saving flows into the USA. Existing balances stay put under their current rules (RMDs, ordinary-income withdrawals) and drain naturally over their holders' lifetimes.

This one move does most of the work. Anyone entering the workforce deals with exactly one account. Payroll, contribution limits, and the entire "which account?" decision simplify immediately. The legacy pile just runs off over the next few decades. The cost is zero and the risk is close to it. Simplification doesn't require a grand migration; it requires closing the door to new complexity.

**Second, an optional conversion, priced honestly.** Anyone who wants to fold their old accounts into their USA can. After-tax accounts (Roth IRAs and 401(k)s, plus 529s, Coverdells, and ABLE accounts) roll in tax-free, since they were funded with after-tax dollars already. Pre-tax accounts (Traditional 401(k), IRA, SEP, SIMPLE) pay tax on the converted balance. Rolled-in balances count against the $5M cap but not the $30,000 annual cap, and the conversion is a separate transition tax that doesn't touch the Lifetime Gains exemption.

The one decision that matters is the conversion rate, and here we break from the usual sales pitch. A conversion "deal" normally dangles a rate below what you'd pay at withdrawal. But that discount isn't free money for the Treasury; it's a loss. Collecting 12% today on a balance that would have been taxed at 30% in twenty years books cash now and gives up more later in present-value terms. A carrot deep enough to convert everyone quickly necessarily deepens the long-run deficit, and that's a line this project won't cross.

So the conversion is priced to be roughly present-value-neutral: the rate is set so the Treasury comes out even between collecting now and collecting later. With no bargain to chase, the people who convert are the ones who genuinely want the simpler life and the certainty of a locked-in rate, not arbitrageurs, and no regressive windfall flows to the largest balances.

It still buys something real. A present-value-neutral conversion pulls revenue *forward*: the government collects tax today that it would otherwise wait decades for, and can retire debt with it now at no net long-run cost. A debt-timing improvement and a faster cleanup, without a giveaway.

> **The conversion rate is a placeholder.** Like the capital-gains numbers in the [Lifetime Gains essay](./lifetime-gains-essay.md), the right figure should come from simulation: model conversion uptake by age, balance, and time horizon, then set the rate at the present-value-neutral point (or wherever a deliberately chosen revenue target lands). Earlier drafts of this proposal floated a flat 12% rate and a $1.2–1.3 trillion one-time haul. Both are retired here, because that framing counted gross cash while quietly spending the future revenue that cash gives up.

There's no deadline and no cliff. Conversion stays open indefinitely; the legacy accounts are simply frozen to new contributions from day one and empty out over time.

## What About the Employer Match?

Here's a thing that sounds heretical until you do the arithmetic: the USA doesn't have an employer match.

Not because matching is evil, but because under a single contribution cap it stops doing anything. Today the match feels like extra tax-advantaged room because it lives in a gap: in 2025 you can defer $23,500 of your own pay into a 401(k), but the *combined* employee-plus-employer limit runs all the way to $70,000. The match fills that $46,500 gap. That's the whole trick. It's bonus shelter for people whose employers are generous and who can already afford to save a lot.

Now collapse everything into one $30,000 cap that counts every dollar regardless of who deposits it. For anyone saving near the cap, an employer dollar just displaces a dollar they'd have put in themselves. The match isn't adding savings capacity anymore; it's relabeling whose money goes in. And $30,000 is already generous. Most workers never get near it. So for almost everyone, "employer match into the USA" is just compensation routed through HR instead of paid as wages.

If it's really just wages, pay it as wages. Let the worker decide where it goes. That's cleaner, it's more honest, and it deletes an entire compliance apparatus: ERISA nondiscrimination testing, top-heavy rules, safe harbor formulas, true-ups, and vesting schedules all evaporate, because there's no employer-owned money sitting in the account waiting to vest. Your USA is yours from dollar one.

There's a fairness argument too, and it's the same one the essay opened with. A match is a savings subsidy you only collect if you have the right employer and enough spare cash to capture it. The people who max it are higher earners at generous firms. The roughly half of workers with no employer plan get nothing. Building the subsidy into the match re-creates exactly the access gap the USA is supposed to close.

The obvious objection: won't saving crater without the "free money, don't leave it on the table" nudge? The evidence says no, or at least not much. Chetty and coauthors studied 41 million people in Denmark and found that about 85% are "passive savers" who barely respond to subsidies at all; each $1 a government spends subsidizing retirement accounts raises total saving by roughly **one cent**, mostly because people just shift money they'd have saved anyway. The 15% who do respond skew wealthier and more financially sophisticated. The thing that actually moves the needle isn't the price subsidy. It's the default.

## The Autopilot Does the Real Work

So keep the part that works. The USA's participation engine isn't the match; it's automatic enrollment plus the $1,000 birth seed.

Auto-enrollment is already federal law in spirit. The SECURE 2.0 Act of 2022 requires most new 401(k) and 403(b) plans to automatically enroll workers at 3% of pay, escalating 1% per year toward a 10% ceiling, with the right to opt out anytime. The USA version is simpler because there's no employer "plan" to sponsor: auto-enrollment becomes a *payroll default*. Any employer that runs payroll defaults new hires into contributing to their existing USA (3% to start, escalating to ~10%, opt out whenever).

That's actually more universal than today's rule, which only fires if your employer bothers to set up a plan. Riding payroll instead of plan sponsorship means the small-shop employee and the gig worker get the same nudge as the Google engineer. The default does the behavioral work the match was pretending to do, without tethering anyone's retirement to the generosity of their boss.

(The 3%-to-10% numbers are stakes in the ground, ported straight from SECURE 2.0. Worth calibrating before this becomes law, not worth bikeshedding now.)

## Won't Some People Lose the Tax Deferral?

The USA is post-tax only. You pay tax on the way in, never on the way out (within the rules above). There's no traditional, pre-tax option.

That costs something real for one group: workers who expect to be in a meaningfully lower tax bracket in retirement than they're in today. Under the current system they can defer at, say, 22% and withdraw at 12%, pocketing the spread. Post-tax-only takes that arbitrage away.

I'm keeping it post-tax anyway, on purpose. The entire premise of this essay is that the Traditional-vs-Roth decision is a tax on financial literacy: a fork most people get wrong, that rewards whoever has the better accountant. You don't simplify that by preserving both options and a calculator to choose between them. You simplify it by picking one. Post-tax wins because it's the honest one. The money in your account is actually yours, with no embedded IOU to the IRS waiting at withdrawal, and the government collects its share now instead of betting on your future bracket. The losers at the margin are real, but they're a narrow band, and the rest of the package (lower and simpler income tax rates, no employee-side FICA) hands most workers more take-home pay to contribute in the first place.

## Won't People Just Squander It? And What Can You Even Hold?

Two related worries: should the government restrict what you can invest in, and should it stop people from blowing the money?

On what you can hold: the USA works like a Roth IRA does today. You can hold basically anything, individual stocks, bonds, mutual funds, ETFs, the whole brokerage menu. The handful of things barred from IRAs now (collectibles, life insurance, your own vacation house and other self-dealing) stay barred. No new whitelist, no committee deciding which index funds are virtuous. Port the existing guardrails and move on. There's no limited employer fund menu here, because there's no employer plan.

On squandering: the cap already handles the only version of this that's society's problem. The $5M ceiling means nobody is sheltering a dynasty in here. Below that, it's your money. A system that lets you withdraw your contributions anytime (a feature, and the whole point) can't also promise you'll never make a bad call. The honest answer to "what if people make mistakes with their own money" is that they sometimes will, and that the alternative (a federal account you can't touch and can't direct) is exactly the paternalism the current 15-account maze already inflicts and that people hate.

The one protection worth building in is the default, not a restriction. The auto-enrolled majority, the ~85% who never actively choose, shouldn't be dropped onto a brokerage screen and told to pick. Default them into a low-cost, age-appropriate index fund (a target-date fund, basically). That's a starting point, not a ceiling: anyone who wants something else can hold whatever the brokerage menu allows, exactly like a Roth today. The passive saver gets a sensible outcome, the active one keeps full run of the account, and nobody's choice gets confiscated.

## What Happens at Death

USA inheritance follows the same rules that govern Roth IRAs under the [SECURE Act of 2019](https://www.congress.gov/bill/116th-congress/house-bill/1994). Porting an established, well-understood rule (rather than inventing a new one) matches the project's commitment to radical simplicity.

- **Surviving spouse.** Treat-as-own rollover. The inherited balance folds into the surviving spouse's USA, continues tax-free for their lifetime, with no required drain. Matches how spousal Roth rollovers work today.
- **Non-spouse heirs.** Inherited balance becomes a separate Inherited USA, which must be fully drained within 10 years of death. Withdrawals during that window are tax-free (the USA is post-tax). No annual required distributions: the heir chooses the pace, including taking the full balance as a lump sum at year 10. Once drained, the dollars enter the heir's taxable brokerage account with basis at fair market value.
- **Eligible Designated Beneficiaries.** Minor children of the decedent, disabled or chronically ill heirs, and any beneficiary not more than 10 years younger than the decedent get a lifetime stretch in place of the 10-year drain. Mirrors current Roth carve-outs.

Net effect: the tax-favored wrapper continues for spouses (lifetime) and for non-spouse heirs (10 years). The 10-year window bounds the continuation rather than perpetuating it across generations. "Same as Roth, applied to USA" is the one-line defense.

## A Few More Questions You Might Have

### "I love my HSA's triple tax advantage. Don't I lose it?"

Partly, yes. The HSA stacks three tax breaks: you deduct contributions going in, the money grows tax-free, and qualified medical withdrawals come out tax-free. The USA keeps two of those (tax-free growth, and tax-free medical withdrawals at any age) and drops the third, the upfront deduction, because the whole system is post-tax. So if you're genuinely running an HSA as a stealth retirement account, you lose a real benefit.

But look at who that actually is. The HSA triple play only works if you (1) are enrolled in a high-deductible health plan, (2) have the cash flow to pay medical bills out of pocket instead of from the account, (3) know to invest the balance instead of letting it sit, and (4) have the discipline to leave it alone for decades. That's a small, sophisticated, mostly higher-income sliver.

The numbers say so. There are about 39 million HSAs in the country, which sounds like a lot until you learn that only about 9% of them, roughly 3.5 million, have any money invested at all. The other 90% are being used as a clunky debit card for this year's copays, which is to say, as a worse FSA. The triple-tax-advantaged wealth machine the rebuttal is defending is something maybe one in a hundred adults actually operates. It's almost a perfect specimen of the financial-literacy tax this whole essay is about: a great deal if you have the right health plan and a good accountant, invisible to everyone else.

And you can't even get one on your own terms. You need a qualifying high-deductible plan first, a coverage decision with real downsides (more out-of-pocket exposure) that nobody should make just to unlock a tax wrapper. Even maxed, an HSA only holds $4,300 a year for an individual ($8,550 for a family). The USA flips all of this: tax-free growth and tax-free medical withdrawals for everyone, up to a $30,000 annual cap, no special health plan required, no brokerage know-how required, no four-step optimization. You give up the deduction on the way in, which was worth the most to the highest earners anyway, and in exchange the benefit stops being a prize for the few who knew to claim it and becomes something every American just has.

### "Isn't a Roth-style account regressive? Tax-free growth helps the rich most."

It's the right worry aimed at the wrong target.

Take the narrow version first, post-tax versus pre-tax. Pre-tax is the regressive one. A deduction is worth your marginal rate, so the same $10,000 traditional contribution cuts a top-bracket saver's taxes by three to four times as much as a middle earner's, same deposit, bigger subsidy the richer you are. Going post-tax deletes that upside-down subsidy. Roth isn't more regressive than traditional; it's less.

The broader version is fair: any tax-favored account helps people who can afford to save, and the rich save more. True. But that's an argument about caps and access, not about Roth, and the USA answers it three ways:

- **The $5M balance cap** bounds accumulation at the top. A tax-free wrapper turns into a rich person's shelter through unlimited growth (see: the multi-billion-dollar Roth IRA). Cap it and that's gone.
- **The flat $1,000 birth seed** is sharply progressive precisely because it's flat: the same grant is a foundation for a poor kid and a rounding error for a rich one.
- **Universality closes the gap the current system widens.** This is the part critics miss. Today's accounts are already regressive, badly. Only 36% of the lowest-paid workers even have access to a workplace retirement plan, versus 91% of the highest paid. Fewer than 3% of families hold a 529, and those that do earn about three times as much as those that don't. The whole apparatus rewards people who know the accounts exist, can guess which one they'll need, and can afford to lock money away. One flexible account every American already has, seeded at birth and auto-enrolled, usable for college or a house or a medical bill or retirement, hands the under-resourced the same wrapper the wealthy already optimize. Against the status quo, consolidating *is* the progressive move.

The cleanest answer is structural, though: the USA is the savings tool, not the redistribution tool. Progressivity lives in the tax brackets and the child payment. Asking one account to also be the welfare system is how you end up with fifteen accounts. Keep the saving simple and capped, and do the redistribution where it belongs.

### "If people can withdraw anytime, won't they just drain it and never retire?"

This worry is backwards. Letting people withdraw their contributions doesn't create a new leak; consolidating into one portable account patches the biggest one we already have.

Leakage is a real and large problem today: Americans pull **$60–105 billion a year** out of retirement accounts early. But the dominant source isn't impulse spending, it's job changes. Between 31% and 47% of people cash out when they leave a job, and most who do drain the whole balance. The reason is friction: your 401(k) is tied to the employer you're leaving, rolling it over is confusing, the balance is often small, so people take the cash and eat the penalty. The USA deletes that trigger entirely. It's one account that follows you across every job, so there's no plan to leave and no rollover to fumble. The single biggest hole in the bucket structurally closes.

The flexibility that remains isn't new, either. "Withdraw your contributions anytime" is exactly how Roth IRA contributions already work. And there's still a brake: your contributions come out free, but gains before age 60 are taxed under the Lifetime Gains rules. The tax-free payoff is the reward for patience — a soft lock, not a cage.

The honest tension is that the same flexibility that makes this account progressive (tap it for a house, a startup, an emergency) is also what lets people drain it. You can't offer penalty-free access and a hard lock at the same time. The bet is that we're better off with people saving who know they can reach the money in a crisis than with people not saving at all for fear they can't. Fear of locking money away is one of the top reasons lower-income households don't save in the first place. An escape hatch is what gets them in the door.

### "Won't Wall Street kill this?"

Some of it will try. But the financial industry isn't a monolith, and the split is telling.

The asset managers, the firms that earn fees on money under management, mostly win. There's about $40 trillion sitting in U.S. retirement accounts today, and a universal account plus auto-enrollment plus a seed for every newborn means more accounts and, over time, more assets to manage. The optional conversion is a fee opportunity on top: helping people consolidate decades of scattered accounts into one is a generation's worth of rollover and advisory work, and a single multi-goal account is a reason to sell each saver several target-date funds instead of one. The wealth-management arms will not be manning the barricades.

The clear losers are narrower: the 401(k) recordkeeping and plan-administration business, which exists to run employer plans, ERISA testing, nondiscrimination rules, safe harbors. The USA deletes the employer plan, so it deletes that work. And the slice of the advice industry that monetizes complexity (Roth or traditional, backdoor conversions, RMD timing) shrinks when there's no maze left to bill against.

Which is the point. The opposition concentrates exactly among the people who profit from the complexity. That's not a flaw in the proposal; it's the proposal working. And it isn't unprecedented: the index-fund revolution and automatic enrollment both reshaped this industry over loud objections, and the firms adapted and grew. The UK and Canada run simple universal accounts alongside thriving financial sectors. Simplicity doesn't kill the industry. It just stops paying it to keep things complicated.

### "With one account for several goals, how do I invest it?"

Real tradeoff, worth naming. A 529 is easy to allocate because every dollar in it is for college, so it can glide toward age 18 on autopilot. A single account holding your house fund, your kid's college fund, and your own retirement has three different time horizons in one pot, and no single glide path serves all three.

The fix is tooling, not structure. You can hold more than one target-date fund in the same account, a 2032 fund for the house down payment, a 2044 fund for college, a 2068 fund for retirement, side by side. Goals-based tools, now standard at every major brokerage and robo-advisor, already do this: you name a goal, a date, and a dollar amount, and the platform allocates across goals inside one account. The mental separation that kept you from raiding the college money becomes a labeled bucket instead of a separate legal vehicle.

So consolidation doesn't erase the allocation decisions; it relocates them into one place where you can see them together and set them at once. That's the upgrade: one portfolio view instead of five. (It's also, not incidentally, a reason the fund industry should warm to this. One account that naturally wants several target-date funds per person is a bigger market for the products they already sell.)

## What's Still Being Calibrated

The architecture is settled. A handful of numbers and edges are stakes in the ground, flagged here honestly rather than buried:

- **Auto-enrollment default rate.** Ported from SECURE 2.0 at 3% escalating to 10%. The right default is an empirical question worth real modeling before enactment.
- **The conversion rate.** Set to roughly present-value-neutral (see "What Happens to the Old Accounts?"), but the actual number needs simulation: model conversion uptake by age, balance, and horizon, then solve for the rate that hits the chosen financial target (PV-neutral, or a deliberately budgeted cost). The old flat-12%/$1.2–1.3T framing is retired.
- **Medical receipt substantiation.** Keeping a tax-free medical withdrawal (and a medical credit in Phase 2) means keeping some version of expense substantiation. The goal is to piggyback on existing insurer and provider reporting (1095s, EOBs) rather than push paperwork onto filers, but the clean mechanism isn't designed yet.
- **State conformity.** Some states tax 529s, HSAs, and retirement accounts on their own terms. Federal reform sets federal treatment only; it doesn't bind the states. The good news is that states overwhelmingly conformed to the federal definitions of Roth IRAs and HSAs, so conformity here is the likely path, not a fight. Out of scope to prescribe, consistent with the project's general deference to state sovereignty.

## Revenue Impact

- **The conversion:** designed to be roughly present-value-neutral, so it isn't a net long-run revenue raiser. What it does is pull revenue *forward* (useful for near-term debt reduction) and migrate willing savers onto one system. The "$1.2–1.3 trillion one-time windfall" framing from earlier drafts is retired; that number was gross cash that ignored the future revenue it spends. The rate is a placeholder pending simulation.
- **Ongoing:** Roughly revenue-neutral. The USA is Roth-like (after-tax contributions, tax-free growth), which means the government collects taxes upfront rather than at withdrawal. This is a timing shift, not a revenue change, except to the extent that the $5M cap prevents unlimited tax-free accumulation.
- **$1,000 seed cost:** ~$3.6 billion per year, paid from general revenue. This is a rounding error: about 0.05% of a ~$7 trillion federal budget, or roughly one part in two thousand. There's no need for a dedicated funding gimmick. Every American child starting life with a funded, compounding account is one of the highest-leverage things this much money can buy.

## Relationship to Other Proposals

- **[Lifetime Gains Framework](./lifetime-gains-essay.md):** The USA's tax treatment of gains (before age 60) uses the Framework's exemption and sliding scale. Rule 5 (Roth Reforms) is fully subsumed by the USA, including its proposed $5M Roth balance cap. There is one balance cap in the system, and it lives in the USA. Rule 5 retires.
- **[Income tax reform](./income-tax-essay.md):** Eliminating deductions and simplifying the rate table reduces the complexity of the Traditional-vs-Roth decision, which the USA eliminates entirely by going post-tax only. The income tax essay's **medical credit** (30% refundable, above a $5,000 threshold) and the USA's tax-free medical withdrawals point at the same expenses, so they can't stack: dollars you cover with a tax-free USA withdrawal don't count toward the medical credit base, and dollars you claim the credit on aren't also pulled tax-free from the USA. One dollar of medical spending, one tax break. This mirrors current law, which already bars deducting expenses paid from an HSA, so it ports cleanly. Enforcement piggybacks on the same insurer and provider reporting the credit relies on.
- **[FICA reform](./fica-reform-essay.md):** Independent, but they compound. FICA elimination raises take-home pay by ~8%, and converting the now-defunct employer match into wages (see above) raises it further. Both land in a worker's paycheck, where the USA's $30K cap and no income limit make saving the easy default rather than an optimization problem.
- **[Child payment](./child-payment-essay.md):** The $1,000 seed and the child payment together form the inbound side of the system architecture: every child starts with a savings account and a monthly payment.

## The Politics

Like the rest of this series, the USA is built to give both sides a win they've wanted for a long time.

**From the right:** One account replaces fifteen. An entire layer of regulation (ERISA testing, nondiscrimination rules, safe harbors, plan compliance) simply disappears. No more required minimum distributions forcing you to drain your own savings. No employer mandate. Every American owns a private, market-based account, seeded at birth, that they control for life. This is the ownership society, actually delivered. And retiring the old accounts pulls tax revenue forward to pay down debt now, without raising a single rate.

**From the left:** Universal access closes a participation gap that is currently brutal (36% of the lowest-paid workers have a retirement plan versus 91% of the highest paid). Every child, regardless of family wealth, starts with capital. Going post-tax kills the upside-down deduction that pays the highest earners the biggest subsidy. The $5M cap shuts the door on billion-dollar Roth shelters. Auto-enrollment reaches everyone, not just the people with generous employers.

**From the middle:** One account, one set of rules, one cap. You can explain it to your neighbor in a sentence. The money is actually yours, with no embedded tax bill waiting at the end and no need to predict at 25 whether you'll need the money for a house, a hospital, or old age. You save. It grows. You use it when you need it.

## What Now

This is a working proposal, not a finished bill. The architecture, one universal account replacing the whole tangle, is the part I'm confident about. The calibration (the exact caps, the conversion rate, the auto-enrollment default) is open, and the modeling on the account conversion still needs real work, which I'd rather say out loud than paper over.

So picture it. You're 22 and starting your first job. You don't have to learn what a 401(k) is, or whether to go Roth or traditional, or what an HSA does, or whether your employer's match has a vesting cliff. You already have one account. It's had a thousand dollars compounding in it since the day you were born. Your paycheck starts feeding it automatically. When you change jobs, nothing happens to it. When you buy a house or hit an emergency or finally retire, the money is there, and it's yours.

That's the whole idea. Fifteen accounts collapse into one, and saving stops being a test of how well you can read the tax code.
