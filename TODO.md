## What if we let AI design and run our tax code?
Sounds crazy ... but we just focus on the philosophy behind it and leave the details of implementation to the borg
Instead of optimizing for proxy variables like Gini coefficients or raw GDP, we've identified two primary system-level goals for a modern tax code:
Dynamic Mobility: Replacing the politically loaded "Meritocracy" with a term that describes a fluid system. It ensures the "Einstein Rubric"—where talent can rise from any birth circumstance while unearned, stagnant wealth is not shielded from the cost of civilization.
Resource Velocity: Replacing "Capital Velocity" to neutralize partisan coding. This frames money as system fuel; the goal is to prevent "clogged" legacy tax shelters and accelerate the flow of resources toward high-risk, high-potential innovation.

## The Philosophy of Luck & Mobility
Money Made = Talent + Effort + Luck.
How much in each?
Luck as a Social Credit: Because innate talent and birth circumstances are forms of "luck" that an individual did not "earn," the resulting windfall is partially a collective credit.
The Insurance Model: Progressive taxation is reframed as a societal insurance policy. You pay more when you are "lucky" to ensure the system remains liquid and supportive for everyone else (including the next Einstein) when luck shifts.

## "No Tax on Tips" case study
Violates horizontal equity (Alice the waiter vs. Bob the cashier, both at $45K, different tax). Creates arbitrage incentives. Right intervention is bracket adjustment, not form-of-income carve-outs. Bigger principle: form of income shouldn't determine rate.

## Student Loan Interest Deduction case study
Behavioral subsidy delivered through definitional mechanism. Phase-outs exclude both very poor and moderately well-off. One of five overlapping higher-ed subsidy mechanisms with zero coordination. Apply gemstone test to entire higher-ed subsidy ecosystem.

## The Withholding Architecture
Withholding is a prediction system structurally guaranteed to be wrong because inputs are too complex to predict. Refunds and tax bills are evidence of the gap between prediction and reality. Connects to USA / post-tax architecture argument.
