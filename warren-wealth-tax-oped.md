# Senator Warren Is Right About the Problem. But the Wealth Tax Debate Is Blocking Real Reform.

*By Matt Sly*

Last week, Senator Elizabeth Warren and 49 Democratic co-sponsors reintroduced the Ultra-Millionaire Tax Act, proposing a 2% annual levy on fortunes above $50 million and 3% above $1 billion. The stated goal: make the ultra-rich pay their fair share. The projected revenue: $6.2 trillion over a decade.

Senator Warren is diagnosing the right disease. Billionaires pay lower effective tax rates than teachers. The "buy, borrow, die" strategy lets the ultra-wealthy live tax-free by borrowing against appreciated assets until death erases the tax bill entirely. They escape the estate tax utilizing trusts and other loophole strategies. This is indefensible.

But a wealth tax is the wrong prescription. There is a radically simpler and more pragmatic approach. And by pushing a wealth tax progressives risk actually protecting the status quo by making real reform less likely.

**We don't need more tax code. We need less.**

The federal tax code and its accompanying regulations span 75,000 pages of interdependent provisions, the accumulated residue of a century of well-intentioned patches. Americans spend $536 billion per year just complying with it. The tax gap (money legally owed but uncollected) exceeds $600 billion annually. That's not an enforcement failure. It's a complexity failure. The IRS literally cannot administer the system we've already built.

A wealth tax adds an entirely new tax type on top of this mess, requiring annual valuation of illiquid assets (private businesses, real estate, art collections) that are expensive, subjective, and gameable to appraise. It creates new planning opportunities for the lawyers who got us here and a new bureaucratic infrastructure the IRS doesn't have. This is the same design pattern that gave us the Alternative Minimum Tax: a "shadow" system bolted on because the primary system doesn't work. The AMT now ensnares millions of upper-middle-class families while the truly wealthy route around it. Adding complexity to fight the consequences of complexity is a losing strategy.

**It won't pass, and that matters.**

Every European country that tried a wealth tax repealed it. France's version drove an estimated 42,000 millionaires out of the country before repeal in 2017. Sweden, Austria, Denmark all reached the same conclusion. The constitutional challenges alone (the Sixteenth Amendment authorizes income taxes, not wealth taxes) would tie this up in courts for years.

Warren knows this. The bill has been introduced since 2019. It has never received a committee vote. At some point, proposing legislation you know won't pass stops being advocacy and starts being an obstacle to reforms that could. Every news cycle spent on a wealth tax is a news cycle not spent closing the loopholes that actually let billionaires pay nothing.

**There's a better way.**

I am a software engineer and entrepreneur. I've built systems used by millions, and I know that complexity compounds and creates unintended consequences. Every patch interacts with every other patch until the system becomes too tangled to fix or enforce. The ultra-wealthy don't pay low rates because income tax rates are too low. They pay low rates because they've found ways to avoid ever realizing income. We don't need another layer. We need to close the exits.

I've proposed a framework built on radical simplification. Instead of a new tax, introduce a lifetime capital gains limit. Every American gets a generous exemption ($2.5 million per person, $5 million per couple). Below that, your gains are tax-free. Above it, gains phase up to ordinary income rates at $10 million. Death and gifts trigger realization, replacing the estate tax with something that actually collects. Borrowing against appreciated assets triggers realization (no more buy-borrow-die). These specific numbers are stakes in the ground, but moving from roughly 25 configuration variables to 2 lets us have a meaningful and predictable debate.

No new tax type. No annual valuations. No constitutional question mark. This framework eliminates the estate tax, the AMT, and roughly 10-15% of the Internal Revenue Code by volume, while generating an estimated $85-200 billion in annual revenue, through mechanisms the IRS already knows how to enforce.

Senator Warren is right that the system is broken. But the answer isn't another patch on a century of patches. It's a refactor. The tax code is America's most expensive technical debt, and you don't fix technical debt by adding more code.
